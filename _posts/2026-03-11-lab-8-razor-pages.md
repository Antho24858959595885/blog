Well, I love Pokémon, so I wanted my own application centered around it. My app stores each Pokémon’s name, type, Pokédex number, and region. The initial tutorial was actually very helpful. Being able to walk through the entire process first made Step 2 feel much more familiar. It was a lot easier than trying to learn and build everything at the same time, and repeating the same structure with my own model helped the steps really click in my head. The biggest struggle was getting scaffolding to work in VS Code. Even though I followed the tutorial exactly (and drove myself a little crazy rechecking the same steps), the CLI version required additional Entity Framework tooling packages before it would generate the CRUD pages. It took some debugging to figure out what was missing and why the generator kept failing. That was definitely the most frustrating part of the lab. Compared to Jekyll, Razor Pages feels much more backend-focused. Jekyll is upfront and content-based, while Razor requires a bit more complexity to get all the pieces to fit. Even if a bit more process based, I feel much more confident creating another Razor Pages application now. I’m excited to see what “much more complicated” looks like. It’s definitely a little intimidating, but I want to do more than just add, edit, and delete.
